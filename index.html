How the Implementation Fee Is Calculated — Your Justification

The implementation fee covers real engineering hours: integration configuration, data migration, location setup, QA testing, and team training. For a typical enterprise deal, this represents 40-80+ hours of dedicated engineering and onboarding time. At blended rates of $150-200/hr for technical implementation resources, the fee target is often at or below our actual cost. This isn't a profit center — it's cost recovery for making sure the platform works on day one.

Customer pushes back on the implementation fee — what's their objection?

💰 "I don't want to pay an implementation fee"

They want to remove it entirely. Do NOT offer to remove it. Explain what happens without proper implementation — slower time-to-value, lower adoption, wasted subscription spend. Walk through the engineering hours and blended rates to make the cost tangible. If they're an SMB (< 10 locs), there is no implementation fee. Otherwise, hold firm.

📉 "I want a lower implementation fee"

They accept the concept but want to negotiate the rate. You have room to negotiate down to the 10% floor — but never lead with it. Start at the segment rate (20% MM, 30% Enterprise) and concede slowly. Below 10% requires Sai approval. Trade: if they want a lower fee, push for a longer contract or faster signature.

Script — "I Don't Want to Pay It"

"I hear you — and I want to make sure I'm being transparent about what this covers. The implementation fee pays for 40-80 hours of dedicated engineering work: integration configuration, data migration, location-by-location setup, QA testing, and team training. At our blended engineering rates, this fee is at or below our actual cost. The customers who skip or minimize this step are the ones who see slower time-to-value and lower adoption in the first 90 days. It's a one-time investment that protects the much larger annual investment you're making in the platform."

Script — "I Want a Lower Fee"

"I can work with you on the number — we have some flexibility. But I want to be upfront: the rate reflects the real engineering work required for an account your size. If you want me to take a lower number to my manager, the strongest case I can make is with a longer contract or a faster signature timeline. That way I'm showing commitment on both sides. What works better for you?"

Script — When They Keep Pushing

"I hear you — it feels like an extra cost on top. But think of it this way: you're paying [ACV] per year for this platform to work. The implementation fee is what makes sure it actually does. We've seen operators try to skip this step and then spend months underutilizing the platform — that's the real cost. If the total contract value is the issue, let's look at contract length or modules — but the implementation fee stays in."

Anchoring Strategy: PLPM vs. Implementation

Customers almost never negotiate both the per-location price and the implementation fee equally hard. Early in the negotiation, figure out which one they care about more. If they're fixated on PLPM, hold firm there and know you have a little room on implementation (above the 10% floor). If they're fixated on implementation, hold that firm and know you can adjust scope or term to move PLPM. The key is: never give ground on both.

The Price / Speed / Certainty Triangle
If They Want Price, We Need Speed and Certainty

Every discount triggers a trade. The customer picks one lever (price), and we get the other two (speed + certainty). Any discount — at any tier — results in a 24-hour deadline to signature. This is non-negotiable. Frame it clearly: "I can work on the price, but I need a commitment that if I get this approved, we're signing within 24 hours. Price, speed, certainty — I can give you price, but I need the other two."

What We Get in Return (Give to Get)

Every concession you make should feel earned. These are the trades that make a discount feel like a partnership, not an extraction. Present these as part of the deal, not as afterthoughts.

24-hour signature deadline
Required on every discount. "I can get you this price, but it's a 24-hour window. If we miss it, we revert to standard."
Customer reference
Agree to be a reference for future prospects in their segment. Zero cost to them, high value to us.
Customer case study
Participate in a written or video case study within 6 months. Great for marketing — makes the discount feel like a partnership.
Multi-year commitment
10–20% discount → suggest 2-year to make manager approval more likely. 20%+ → 3-year or 4-year is the only path to CEO approval.
Platform tier upgrade
Upgrade from Standard to Plus or Pro. Higher CSM investment = higher retention and expansion potential.
Additional locations
Expand location count in exchange for better per-location rate. Increases total ACV even at a discount.
Script — The Give to Get

"I can work on the pricing for you — but here's what I'd need in return. First, if I get this approved, we're signing within 24 hours. Second, I'd love for you to be a reference customer for us — we'll ask you to hop on a call with a prospect or two in your segment, and ideally participate in a case study down the road. That way this feels like a partnership, not just a discount. Fair?"

🚫 What To NEVER Do
BOTH
▾
✗Never offer a discount before they ask for one. You're training them to always expect more.
✗Never say "I can give you X% off" as an opener. You've negotiated against yourself.
✗Never give a discount without getting something in return. A trade makes the discount feel earned, not extracted.
✗Never promise a discount that requires approval before you have it. It destroys your credibility if it falls through.
✗Never let a discount conversation die in email. Call them — urgency dies in async.
✗Never negotiate both PLPM and implementation fee. Figure out which one the customer cares about and anchor there.
Core Principle — Annual Is Standard, Multi-Year Is the Reward

Annual contracts paid upfront are the baseline for every deal — new and renewal. Multi-year is a tool, not the default. For new business, it unlocks bigger discounts. For renewals, it gives existing customers price protection and a 10% incentive on a 3-year commitment.

Script — Linking Discount to Contract Length (New Business)

"I hear you on the pricing — and I want to find a way to make this work. Here's the reality: a discount at this level needs manager approval, and the strongest case I can make is with a 2-year commitment. You'd take today's pricing and lock it for the full term — no increases for 24 months. If I go to my manager with a 1-year deal at this price, it's going to be a much harder conversation. Which direction makes more sense?"

"I want to be transparent — a discount at this level requires our CEO's direct approval. The only scenario where he's likely to say yes is a 3-year (or longer) commitment. At that level, you lock in today's pricing for the full term — no increases. I need to bring this to him within 24 hours — if he approves, are you ready to commit to a 3-year?"

CSM
▾

For renewals, multi-year serves two purposes: it gives the customer price protection from the 7% annual increase, and it unlocks a discount incentive for longer commitment.

+3%

No Approval Needed at Standard Rates

The rates above (1yr +7%, 2yr +3%, 3yr -10%) are pre-approved. CSMs can offer these without escalation. Approval is only required if the customer wants a better deal than these standard rates — e.g., 0% on a 1-year, or more than 10% off on a 3-year.

Positioning the Renewal Tiers

Lead with the 1-year at 7% — it's the standard and most customers accept it. If they push back on price, the 2-year at 3% is your first move: "Instead of 7%, I can get you to 3% if we do a 2-year. That's less than inflation." If they push harder or threaten to leave, the 3-year at -10% is the retention play: "If you commit to 3 years, your rate actually goes down 10% from where you are today." Always present these as a ladder, not a menu.

Script — Positioning the 3-Year Renewal

"I want to put something on the table. If you commit to a 3-year renewal, your rate actually goes down 10% from where you are today — and it's locked for the full 36 months. Compare that to three 1-year renewals where you'd see a 7% increase each time. On a deal your size, the difference is significant. Is that something worth exploring?"

Script — Proactive Price-Lock

"One thing I want to flag — our standard renewal is a 7% increase on a 1-year. But if we do a 2-year, that drops to just 3% — and it's locked for 24 months. On a 3-year, your rate actually goes down 10%. Want me to model what each option looks like for your account?"

Script — Customer Pushing Hard on Price

"I hear you — and I want to find a path that works. On a 1-year, the standard is a 7% increase. But if we do a 2-year, I can bring that down to 3% and lock it. And if you're open to a 3-year, your rate actually drops 10% from today — locked for 36 months. I don't even need special approval for those rates. Want me to model all three?"

⚡ Creating Urgency — Getting Them to Sign Quickly
AE
▾

Urgency isn't about pressure — it's about helping the customer understand the real cost of waiting. Every day they delay is a day their locations run without the platform. The best AEs don't create urgency artificially; they make the customer feel the gap between where they are and where they could be.

Quantify the Cost of Inaction

Before any urgency tactic, calculate what the customer is losing daily by not being on the platform. Missed reviews, unresponded customer issues, manual workflows — put a dollar figure on it. "Every week you wait, your team is spending [X hours] on manual review management across [Y locations]. That's [$ amount] in labor alone, before you factor in the revenue impact of unresponded negative reviews. The sooner we get you live, the sooner that cost disappears."

Onboarding Capacity Is Real (Not Manufactured)

Our implementation and onboarding team has finite capacity. Be specific: "Our onboarding team runs in cohorts — the next one kicks off [date] with [X] slots remaining. If we miss that window, the next cohort is [later date], which delays your go-live by [X weeks]. I'm holding a spot for you, but I can't guarantee it past [deadline]." This works because it's true — and you should confirm slot availability with your CSM before using this.

Pricing Protection Window

Use the 7% annual increase as a real urgency driver: "Our standard annual price adjustment is 7%. If we close this before [quarter end / rate card review], you lock in the current pricing. After that, I genuinely can't guarantee these numbers — and on a [X]-location deal, even a small rate change adds up fast." Make the math tangible with their specific location count.

CSM Match Quality

The right CSM match matters — and availability is real: "I've identified [CSM name] as the ideal match for your account — they've worked with [similar operators / same segment] and know your use case deeply. Their book is filling up, and if we wait past [date], I'll likely need to assign someone else. I'd rather get you the best fit." Only use this when you've actually pre-identified the CSM.

Seasonal Readiness

For restaurants and QSRs, timing around peak seasons is critical: "If you're targeting [summer / holiday season / spring], we need to start implementation now. Our standard onboarding is [X weeks], and you want the platform fully dialed in before your busiest period — not during it. Operators who launch mid-season always wish they'd started earlier." Map their business calendar and work backward from their peak.

The 24-Hour Sai Rule (AE Alignment)

For any deal requiring special approval: "I need to take this to Sai within 24 hours — he's our CEO and he reviews these personally. Honestly, you and I are on the same page here. Let me go to bat for you, but I need your commitment that if he approves this, we're signing. Can you give me that?" This positions Sai as the gatekeeper and aligns the AE with the customer — you're fighting for them, not against them. The 24-hour window is real and creates natural momentum.

The "Dead Deal" Anti-Pattern

Urgency only works if the deal is alive. If you've gone more than 2 weeks without substantive contact, urgency tactics feel manipulative. Before deploying any urgency lever, ask yourself: "Is this customer actively engaged, or am I trying to resuscitate a dead deal?" If it's the latter, re-engage on value first — urgency on a cold deal pushes people away.

🧠 The Best-in-Class AE Mindset
AE
▾
Assume the Close

Write proposals as if they're signing. Present timelines, kickoff dates, onboarding steps. Confidence is contagious.

Urgency Must Be Real

Never manufacture fake urgency. Real urgency: onboarding cohorts, CSM availability, pricing windows, quarter-end bandwidth.

Silence Is a Weapon

After presenting the offer, stop talking. The first person who speaks gives ground. Get comfortable with 3–5 seconds of silence.

Give to Get

Every concession should cost them something. Even a small trade — reference, case study, faster signature — changes the dynamic.

Go High Early

Always start at full price with standard fees and the segment implementation rate (20% Mid-Market, 30% Enterprise). Starting at a discount signals you don't believe in your own pricing. Remember: you have 0–10% discretion — save it for the close, not the open.

Bounded Choices

"Do you want to go with the 1-year or 2-year?" Not "What do you think?" Bounded choices accelerate decisions.

🔄 CSM-Specific: Renewals & Expansion
CSM
▾
Start Renewals 90 Days Early

Never be in a renewal conversation with less than 60 days runway. Urgency dies, alternatives get explored, and budget cycles get complicated. 90 days = leverage. And remember: the 7% increase is coming — use it to drive multi-year conversations early.

Script — Proactive Renewal Opener (90 Days Out)

"I wanted to get ahead of your renewal — we have about 90 days, which is the right time to talk about what's working and what the next 12–24 months could look like. I should mention upfront that our standard renewal includes a 3–7% adjustment. The good news is, if we do a multi-year renewal, we apply the increase once and then lock that rate for the full term — no further increases during the contract. Can we block 30 minutes to walk through the options?"

Expand Before You Renew

A customer who just added modules has much higher commitment to renewal. A customer debating renewal is a much harder sell for expansion. Always expand first.

Script — Expansion Pitch (CSM)

"Based on what I've seen in your account, [LM / next module] would directly address [specific pain point we've discussed]. I can put together a quick add-on quote — it's actually cheaper per location to add it now than to price it separately at renewal. Want me to run the numbers?"

Platform Fee Upgrade at Renewal

Renewal is the natural moment to upgrade CSM tiers. If the customer has been on Standard and you've been doing more informal work, formalize it. "You've been getting shared CSM-level attention on a Standard plan — let's make it official with Plus or Pro so you're fully supported. Most operators your size are on Pro with an Enterprise CSM."
